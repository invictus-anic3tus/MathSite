Arithmetic

Arithmetic: The branch of mathematics dealing with numbers and basic operations like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

Integer: A whole number that can be positive, negative, or zero, but not a fraction or decimal.

Prime Number: A number greater than 1 that has no divisors other than 1 and itself.

Factorial: The product of all positive integers less than or equal to a given number (denoted as n!). For example, 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120.

Natural Numbers: Positive whole numbers starting from 1 (e.g., 1, 2, 3...). Sometimes includes 0 depending on the context.

Rational Number: A number that can be expressed as a fraction where both the numerator and denominator are integers, and the denominator is not zero.

Irrational Number: A number that cannot be expressed as a fraction, such as √2 or π, with non-repeating, non-terminating decimals.

Greatest Common Divisor (GCD): The largest number that divides two or more integers without leaving a remainder.

Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest multiple that two or more numbers share.

Absolute Value: The distance of a number from 0 on the number line, always non-negative. For example, |−5| = 5.

Geometry

Geometry: The branch of mathematics concerned with the properties and relations of points, lines, surfaces, and solids.

Angle: The figure formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint, measured in degrees or radians.

Parallel Lines: Lines in a plane that never meet, no matter how far they extend.

Perpendicular Lines: Lines that intersect to form a 90-degree angle.

Polygon: A closed, flat shape with straight sides. Examples include triangles, quadrilaterals, pentagons, and hexagons.

Pythagorean Theorem: In a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (longest side) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (a² + b² = c²).

Congruent Figures: Figures that are identical in shape and size, even if rotated or flipped.

Area: The measure of the amount of space inside a 2D shape. For example, the area of a rectangle is length × width.

Volume: The measure of space inside a 3D object, such as a cube or cylinder.

Tangent: A line that touches a curve at exactly one point without crossing it.

Algebra

Algebra: The branch of mathematics dealing with symbols and rules for manipulating those symbols, representing numbers and relationships.

Variable: A symbol (often a letter) used to represent an unknown value in an equation or expression.

Equation: A statement that two expressions are equal, often containing one or more variables to solve for.

Polynomial: An algebraic expression consisting of terms that are constants or variables raised to whole-number powers.

Expression: A combination of numbers, variables, and operations without an equals sign (e.g., 3x + 2).

Linear Equation: An equation of the form ax + b = 0, where the graph is a straight line.

Quadratic Equation: An equation of the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where the graph is a parabola.

Absolute Value Equation: An equation involving the absolute value of a variable, such as |x − 3| = 7.

Inequality: A mathematical statement that uses symbols like <,>, ≤, or ≥ to compare expressions.

Exponential Function: A function of the form f(x) = a·b^x, where b > 0 and b ≠ 1, often modeling growth or decay.

Logarithm: The inverse of an exponential function; if b^y = x, then log_b(x) = y.

Linear Algebra

Matrix: A rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns, used in linear algebra to solve systems of equations or transformations.

Vector: A quantity with both magnitude and direction, represented by an arrow in geometry or as an array of numbers in algebra.

Determinant: A scalar value that can be computed from the elements of a square matrix, used to determine whether a matrix is invertible.

Eigenvalue: A scalar that indicates how much a corresponding eigenvector is scaled during a linear transformation.

Row Reduction: The process of using elementary row operations to simplify a matrix, often to row-echelon form.

Rank: The number of linearly independent rows or columns in a matrix.

Span: The set of all possible linear combinations of a set of vectors.

Orthogonal Vectors: Two vectors that have a dot product of zero, indicating they are perpendicular.

Diagonalization: The process of finding a diagonal matrix similar to a given square matrix, using its eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

Transformation: A function mapping vectors from one space to another, often represented by a matrix.

Calculus

Calculus: A branch of mathematics focusing on limits, functions, derivatives, integrals, and infinite series.

Derivative: A measure of how a function changes as its input changes, representing the slope of the function at any given point.

Integral: A fundamental concept in calculus representing the area under a curve or the accumulation of quantities.

Limit: The value that a function or sequence approaches as the input or index approaches some value.

Chain Rule: A rule for finding the derivative of a composite function: if y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) * g'(x).

Product Rule: A rule for differentiating the product of two functions: (uv)' = u'v + uv'.

Quotient Rule: A rule for differentiating the division of two functions: (u/v)' = (u'v − uv') / v².

Critical Point: A point on a graph where the derivative is zero or undefined, often indicating a local maximum, minimum, or inflection point.

Asymptote: A line that a curve approaches but never touches as the curve extends to infinity.

Definite Integral: Represents the area under a curve between two bounds, often written as ∫[a,b] f(x) dx.

Indefinite Integral: Represents the general antiderivative of a function, written as ∫f(x) dx + C.

Calculus II

Calculus II: An advanced branch of calculus dealing with infinite series, parametric equations, polar coordinates, and differential equations.

Taylor Series: A series representation of a function as an infinite sum of terms based on its derivatives at a single point: \( f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x - a)^n \).

Maclaurin Series: A special case of the Taylor Series centered at \( a = 0 \): \( f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!} x^n \).

Polar Coordinates: A coordinate system where each point is represented by its distance \( r \) from the origin and angle \( \theta \) from the positive x-axis.

Parametric Equations: A method of defining a curve by expressing coordinates \( x \) and \( y \) as functions of a parameter \( t \): \( x = f(t), y = g(t) \).

Arc Length (Parametric): The length of a curve given by parametric equations: \( L = \int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} \, dt \).

Polar Integration: The area enclosed by a polar curve \( r = f(\theta) \): \( A = \frac{1}{2} \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \left[f(\theta)\right]^2 \, d\theta \).

Laplace Transform: A technique for solving differential equations by transforming them into algebraic equations: \( \mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-st}f(t) \, dt \).

First-Order Differential Equation: A differential equation involving the first derivative of the unknown function: \( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \).

Second-Order Differential Equation: A differential equation involving the second derivative of the unknown function: \( a\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + b\frac{dy}{dx} + cy = 0 \).
